HC 3: NYC CLean Heat program

New York City is famous for its tall buildings with lights bright enough to see from outer space. Less well known and equally less glamorous, New York City is also a large consumer of nonrenewable fuel used to heat homes for the millions residing in the city. The need for cleaner air and cleaner use of energy is only becoming more important with the current climate of the world. As a result, the demand for programs that can provide clean heating fuels for this city has consequently increased. Programs have been put into action to deal with this issue. The NYC Retrofit Accelerator has partnered with the NYC Clean Heat Program with the aim to make buildings more energy-efficient. By 2015, the program has helped over 5,000 buildings convert to cleaner energy, giving NYC the cleanest air its had in fifty years. Greener Greater Buildings is the United State’s most ambitious set of energy efficiency laws focused on New York City’s largest buildings. GGB requires the buildings to benchmark energy and water use with public disclosure annually, hoping that the transparency will result in more careful use of energy and water. In order to better understand the programs, it is important to note the fuels used and their pros and cons. No. 4 and No. 6 fuels, also known as heavy or residual fuels, are, although inexpensive and high energy, fuels that contribute a lot to air pollution. The DEP Boiler Registration, short for Department of Environmental Protection Boiler Registration, is a program designed to regulate the operation of boilers within a given jurisdiction. All of these programs and increasing aware of the problems are signs that New York is moving towards a more environmentally sound future.

HC 4: NYC Emissions

Queens, located in the heart of New York City, is a sprawling borough known for its immense cultural diversity and vibrant communities. Queens boasts a multifaceted urban environment, characterized by an eclectic mix of building types, ranging from towering residential structures to sprawling commercial complexes. These architectural marvels, while symbolizing the borough’s dynamism, also account for a significant share of its carbon footprint. According to data sourced from the New York City Mayor’s Office of Sustainability, buildings in the city at large are responsible for an astonishing 68% of greenhouse gas emissions. Manhattan, the bustling core of the city, records the highest emissions, with carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions from buildings and transportation reaching 35 million metric tons in 2020, the highest among the boroughs. Brooklyn, in contrast, has seen notable reductions in emissions over the past decade, with a significant decrease in CO2 emissions by approximately 15% from 2010 to 2020, thanks to energy-efficient building upgrades and a growing preference for public transportation. Queens grapples with emissions challenges arising from suburban areas and industrial zones, with CO2 emissions of 26 million metric tons in 2020, showing moderate progress through cleaner technologies and sustainable urban planning. The Bronx, once burdened with environmental disparities, has shown commendable improvements in air quality and emissions reductions, with CO2 levels decreasing by 12% from 2010 to 2020, primarily due to initiatives like tree planting and stricter industrial regulations.
