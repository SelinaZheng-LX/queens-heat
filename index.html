Queens needs to and is moving to greener heat...

As Queens grapples with its substantial carbon footprint of 58 megatons of CO2, it faces a crucial pivot toward energy-efficient upgrades and renewable energy adoption to mitigate its impact on the environment.These initiatives signal a growing momentum towards eco-conscious decisions, paving the way for a more sustainable future in this diverse and vibrant borough. The neighborhoods in Queens we analyzed included Ridgewood, Astoria, Douglaston-Little Neck, Briarwood, and Forest Hills (check out each neighborhood's data by clicking on the neighborhood above).

No.4 and Nov.6 Fuels

These predominantly residential neighborhoods showcase ethnic diversity, boast a high average high school graduation rate, and primarily feature an income distribution under $50,000, representing a mix of working-class and middle-class residents. Within our research in Queens, we reached a significant conclusion. Most energy usage is not because of transportation, but because of elevator apartments. The only type of building closest would be walk-up apartments, however, these mostly use No. 4 fuels. Now, elevator apartments use a nearly equal amount of No. 4 and No. 6 fuels, which is much higher than other apartments. These fuels devastate the environment because of harmful chemicals they emit such as sulfur when burned. Alas, we are dependent on these fuels to provide energy to our buildings.

Goal to stay green...

According to the NYC Clean Heat dataset, Ridgewood, Astoria, and Douglaston-Little Neck lean towards No. 4 fuels due to a comparatively higher income level. Briarwood, with its lower income status, predominantly utilizes No. 6 fuels. Forest Hills displays a balanced fuel ratio. These neighborhoods reflect distinct social and economic landscapes, influencing their choices in heating fuels and environmental impact. Still, with incentives and increased transparency with building owners and the public under the NYC Clean Heat program, we will be able to combat energy usage and ensure New York stays green.
